# 2023-03-24-User EmpowermentGlass Half Full: 

Users cannot imagine being empowered.

Users cannot imagine being empowered because they are given a paucity of choices by developers.

Developers provide a paucity of choices because they have been indoctrinated into a single-paradigm philosophy - FP, over-emphasis on "efficiency".  There are several kinds of "efficiency", but, only one is emphasized: the efficiency of end-user applications.

Lisp, Smalltalk, JavaScript, etc. "feel" free-er because they allow thinking in different paradigms. They are dynamic languages which do not cause mind-lock on "efficiency" before "design". In that sense, Lisp is better than Smalltalk and is better than JavaScript, because Lisp allows multi-paradigm thinking, but, Lisp can be too verbose in any single paradigm.

This, of course, leads to the conclusion that Assembler is better than Lisp and, further, that VLSI is better than Assembler, and, that molecules are better than VLSI, and, that atoms are better than molecules.  

Paradigms are simplifying assumptions and must be chosen to fit the problem-at-hand.  A real solution may consist of many paradigms.

Corollary: using a single-paradigm programming language causes mind-lock.

One must remember the limitations of any particular simplifying assumption before applying the simplifying assumption.

Lisp *restarts* exist "only" in Lisp.  The very idea was chopped off at the knees by premature optimization (aka FP).

Can you express flexible constructs in single-paradigm, efficiency-first languages?  

Yes.

Can you think freely enough to invent flexible constructs in single-paradigm, efficiency-first languages?  

Yes, but, it is 10x[^x] easier using multi-paradigm thinking.

Developers tend to ignore more-difficult-to-express design concepts.  This leads to a paucity of choices for end-users.

[^x]: 10x, 100x, 1000x?  I'm pulling numbers out of thin air to emphasize "efficiency" in the dimension of *development*.

The concept of "macros" was invented in Lisp, not Racket, not Haskell, not Rust, etc.  Racket, Haskell, Rust, etc. allowed buffing the concept of "macros" but did not encourage the actual invention of "macros".

I argue that there is a "tipping point" which denies or encourages certain design choices, and, that all of our popular programming languages discourage a vast number of design choices. The "tipping point" is subtle.  

For example, I hate mind-mapping tools, except Scapple and Kinopio, due to an aggregate of seemingly small differences.

Another example: I like Lisp more than most programming languages because I am allowed to invent things and to easily discard things which turn out to be stupid. When I used C, though, I would be forced to make up my mind early, and found it undesirable to back up and try again.

Programming consists of 2 activities: 
1. design 
2. production engineering.  

(1) has been vastly overlooked, while (2) dominates the so-called "programming" space.

Being able to measure something is not the same as being able to design something. Measurement typically attacks the low-hanging fruit but leaves many interesting concepts alone.  

The best-known measurement of UX is Jef Raskin's "The Humane Interface".  In that book, he does not attack the concepts of improving low-level CPU efficiency.  Raskin talks mostly about improving UXs at levels well above those of low-level CPU efficiency.

Vinyl sounds better than MP3s.  We know how to measure frequencies which leads us to conclude that MP3s are "better" than vinyl. At least one vital aspect of UX - "sounding better" - remains unmeasured.  The reality of "sounding better" consists of a number of factors.  Crushing all of the factors down into a single 2D plot does not address the reality of "sounding better".

"Conspiracy theorists" discuss aspects of 4D electrical circuits that are ignored by 2D equations and oscilloscopes. They call this "free energy".  4D encompasses the dimensions of x/y/z/t, whereas most electrical equations are only 2D -  x/y.
