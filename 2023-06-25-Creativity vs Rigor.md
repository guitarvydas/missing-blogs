# 2023-06-25-Creativity vs RigorThe more you know, the more creative you can be (paraphrased from private conversation with Pat Pattison).  

There are at least 2 ways to know more
1. ad-hoc application of experience
2. being taught what the parts are.  

(1) is what "artists" do, while (2) is how academics take ideas apart and pass them down to students.  

Taking ideas apart requires that one be very, very explicit, and, usually requires that labels be applied to the explicit bits and pieces so that they can be talked about[^ds].

[^ds]: I find that Denotational Semantics is a very, very explicit explanation of computer programs, while FP (Functional Programming) is not.  FP elides/hides certain aspects of computer hardware, like the callstack, which makes its easier to explain certain things, but makes it harder to explain other things.  Over-use of FP - as a Silver Bullet - results in epicycles and bloatware when the technique is mis-applied to concepts that fall out of the sweet spot for FP (like preemption, side-effects, etc.).

I've tried both approaches. I find that technique (2) can sometimes result in helpful revelations about creative, new combinations of concepts.

It is difficult to explain what you know in your bones.  So-called artists tend to avoid explaining their work and just create things with their deep knowledge.  So-called academics tend to dig deeply into tiny slices of understanding and try to explain what they know about their own deep knowledge.

Academic disassembly of ideas is ad-hoc, too, regardless of how anti-ad-hoc academics think their process is.

Academics "choose" to disassemble and explain only certain ideas - ones that appeal to them for one reason or another. i.e. the choice is ad-hoc and based on essentially-random appeal.

You can't explain *everything*, so you choose to explain only a single slice of reality.  That choice is ad-hoc.

An Artist creating an oil painting, also, chooses to explain only a slice of reality.  The Artist uses different techniques to create explanations, e.g. the way that a brush is held and stroked, etc.  It takes years of intense study to be able to explain slices of reality using nuanced detail. 

Academics are "artists", too, albeit, using a different medium (words - technical jargon - typically).  These layers tend to be orthogonal to those usually associated with Art.

I.E. rigorous, detailed explanation of a certain idea is Art, too. But, we tend to use a different label for it.
