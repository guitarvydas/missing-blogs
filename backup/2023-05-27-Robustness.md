# 2023-05-27-Robustness1. scenario A
	1. you walk into Radio Shack and buy a calculator for $7.99
	2. you leave Radio Shack with your new calculator
	3. you type in "2+2" into the calculator, the 1-line displays blinks then shows "4"
	4. you type in "2+3", the display shows "5"
	5. you type in "2+4", the display goes blank
	6. you go back to Radio Shack and demand a replacement, since you have determined that the caculator is faulty
2. scenario A'
	2. you leave Radio Shack with your second new calculator
	3. you type in "2+2" into the calculator, the 1-line displays blinks then shows "4"
	4. you type in "2+3", the display shows "5"
	5. you type in "2+4", the display shows "7"
	6. you think about it and notice that something ain't right
	7. you go back to Radio Shack and demand a replacement, since you have determined that the caculator is faulty
3. scenario A''
	2. you leave Radio Shack with your third new calculator
	3. you get answers "4", "5", "6" as expected
	4. you assume that this, third, calculator is not faulty and you do your taxes with it

I guess that what I'm asking is about my feeling that software-based products that use the "accepted norm" of development, have not progressed much beyond the above scenarios.

I *think* that game developers and hardware developers used to develop products in a manner different from the "accepted norms".  Today, though, I *think* that game developers and hardware developers have been adopting techniques from the "accepted norm" with the result that software and hardware is becoming buggier - from the end-users' perspective.

Games might have twisty game logic, but, overall the software/hardware substrates that games run on tend to be stable and don't crash mid-game.

I'm not a gamer and I can only observe from afar.
